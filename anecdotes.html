A Cathedral Shaped by Centuries

Leicester Cathedral has stood as a symbol of community and faith for many centuries. Its medieval tower and stonework have watched the city grow and change around it, while the building continues to welcome new generations of visitors.

Beneath the surface, however, the ground preserves much older traces of the people who lived, worked and worshipped on this site long before today’s Cathedral took shape.

Opening Up the Cathedral Site

The excavations began with the careful removal of modern surfaces and rubble around the Cathedral. Archaeologists and contractors worked together to strip back the upper layers so that earlier deposits could be reached safely.

At this stage the area can look like a building site, but every layer of soil has the potential to contain clues about Leicester’s past. Once the ground is opened up, archaeologists can decide where to dig more deeply and start to uncover the stories hidden beneath the surface.

What Lies Beneath: Excavations by University of Leicester Archaeological Services (ULAS)

As work went deeper, the excavations revealed walls, grave cuts and other structures that had been buried for centuries. Layers of burials, building foundations and later alterations show how the churchyard and Cathedral were rebuilt and reused over a long period.

Alongside these larger features, the soil also produced smaller finds such as pottery, animal bone and oyster shell. Together, these remains help archaeologists to reconstruct everyday life around the Cathedral and to explain how the site developed into the building visitors see today.

More Stories Beneath the Surface

Inside the Cathedral, stained glass windows show scenes of faith, local history and everyday human experience. Coloured light and patterned glass turn stories into images, just as the archaeology outside turns traces in the soil into stories about real people.

Together, the windows above and the layers below remind visitors that Leicester Cathedral is both a place of worship and a place of memory, where new generations can explore the past.

Continuing the Cathedral’s Story

The reinterment of King Richard III in 2015 brought worldwide attention to Leicester Cathedral and to the city’s archaeological work. His tomb inside the Cathedral is now a major focus for visitors, linking national history with the local community.

The Cathedral remains a living place, shaped by its long past and by the work of archaeologists, conservators and educators today. The stories uncovered in the ground now feed into family activities such as “Cathedral Kids: The Big Dig”, helping children discover the history beneath their feet.
